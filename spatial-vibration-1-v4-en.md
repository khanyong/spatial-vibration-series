## Chapter 3. Mathematical Formulation
This chapter mathematically proves how the Schrödinger equation transforms into a 'spatial vibration equation' describing physical reality.

### 3.1. Polar Transformation
Defining the Schrödinger equation:
$$ i\hbar \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial t} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla^2 \psi + V\psi $$

$$ \psi(\mathbf{r}, t) = R(\mathbf{r}, t) e^{iS(\mathbf{r}, t)/\hbar} $$

### 3.2. Equation Decomposition and Derivation
Substituting $\psi$ into the Schrödinger equation and separating the real and imaginary parts yields:

**[Real Part: Quantum Hamilton-Jacobi Equation]**
Rearranging the real part yields a form where a quantum perturbation term is added to the classical Hamilton-Jacobi equation.
$$ \frac{\partial S}{\partial t} + \frac{(\nabla S)^2}{2m} + V - \frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{\nabla^2 R}{R} = 0 $$
In this model, the added term is defined as the **'Spatial Vibration Potential ($Q_s$)'**. While its mathematical structure is identical to the quantum potential in Bohm's hidden-variable theory [3], this study completely redefines it not as an unknown internal force, but as the **'geometrical fluctuation energy of the background space itself'**.
$$ Q_s \equiv -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{\nabla^2 R}{R} $$
$$ \frac{\partial S}{\partial t} + \frac{(\nabla S)^2}{2m} + V + Q_s = 0 $$

**[Imaginary Part: Continuity Equation]**
Rearranging the imaginary part yields a continuity equation indicating the conservation of vibration energy density ($R^2$) in space.
$$ \frac{\partial R^2}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot \left( R^2 \frac{\nabla S}{m} \right) = 0 $$
